Learning to defeat cancer

Deep Learning for Cancer Genomics

Cancer is the consequence of sequential mutations occuring during the lifetime. Advances in sequencing technology have commoditized genome wide sequencing of tumours. The genome wide profile of mutations can be determined for a few hundred dollars and this cost is ever decreasing to the extent that within the next decade it is likely genome wide profiling will form a standard component of the assessment for a newly diagnosed cancer. The challenge therefore is to leverage this information to determine prognosis and guide optimal treatment.

For many cancers, the gold standard for prediction of prognosis and decisions as to treatment remains the examination of microscope slides prepared from the tumour. For example, the prognosis and treatment of malignant melanoma - one of the most common forms of cancer to affect young adults - is determined primarily by the depth that the tumour has invaded within the skin, the number of dividing cells seen on the slide and whether the cancer has spread to the lymph nodes or beyond.

Every cancer is different. Tumours typically contain thousands of novel mutations. Some of these may contribute to disease progression whereas others occur by chance and do not play a direct role in development of the tumour. Furthermore, there are hundreds of ways in which any individual gene or pathway can be disrupted. Taming this complexity represents an enormous technological and computational challenge. Our approach is to apply Deep Learning approaches to understand the patterns of mutations and pathways within tumours at a higher level. We believe that this will lead to more accurate methods of predicting the behaviour of a tumour, that it will guide treatment decisions and that it will allow better stratification of cancers within clinical trials.

Novel targeted cancer therapeutics and immunotherapies represent one of the most exciting areas in modern medicine. However the cost of bringing a treatment to market typically runs to billions of dollars with many therapies failing to demonstrate efficacy at late stages of development. Our technology permits more effective stratification of tumours reducing the risk of costly failure during clinical trials and more effectively permitting the identification of patients who will benefit from a new treatment.

Prognosis

Most cancers kill by spreading elsewhere within the body. The ability to predict the liklihood that a cancer has spread dictates the requirements for future surveillance and invasive investigations.

Treatment stratification

More effective stratification of tumours will facilitate a personalized approach to treatment selection.

Novel therapies

We will partner with pharmaceutical companies to stratify tumour subtypes that respond to novel therapies reducing the risk of failure during clinical trials.
